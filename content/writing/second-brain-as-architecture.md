Most personal knowledge systems fail in the same place. Not at capture — _capturing is easy_ — but at the architecture that has to outlast the enthusiasm of the first month.

The right reference, I think, is not productivity literature. It is residential architecture: rooms used differently in different seasons, materials chosen for how they age, storage designed for the boring, repetitive habits of an ordinary week.

## Three principles I follow

**Plain materials.** Markdown, plain text, open formats. Anything that depends on a specific tool I treat as a temporary tenant.

**Few rooms, used often.** Five top-level folders, not fifty. The taxonomy should be visible from the doorway.

**Daily entry, weekly cleaning.** The journal is the front door. The weekly review is the broom by it.

The result is not exciting. It is a quiet system that does the work of remembering for me. Which, increasingly, feels like the most luxurious thing a piece of software can offer.
