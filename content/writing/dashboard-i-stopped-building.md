Halfway through 2025 I deleted a dashboard I had spent three months building. It had every conceivable filter. It loaded in under a second. It was, by every measure I had been taught to value, _good_.

Nobody had opened it in six weeks.

In its place I wrote a three-page brief, in plain prose, sent every Monday morning. It took longer to write than the dashboard had taken to build. People read it on the train. They argued with it in the margins. It was, finally, useful.

## The substitution

A dashboard answers questions that have already been asked. A brief teaches the reader which questions are worth asking next. The first is a tool; the second is closer to a colleague.

The substitution is uncomfortable for the analyst. Briefs do not scale. They cannot be A/B tested. They require an opinion. They expose the writer in a way that a chart never quite does.

But they get read.

## What I keep

I still build the warehouse. I still write the queries. I still maintain a handful of dashboards for the operational teams who genuinely need them — the kind of person who needs to know, right now, whether the pipeline has caught up.

For everyone else, I write the brief. Slowly. Edited by hand. Sent on time. Three pages, never four.
