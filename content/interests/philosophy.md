René Girard sits at the centre of how I think about desire and action. The core insight, that most of what we want we want because someone else wants it first, is not depressing. It's operationally useful. The discipline is catching the mechanism in real time, in myself, before mimetic conviction starts to masquerade as my own judgement. Three domains where I run the test honestly. Academic credentials. Professional positioning. Watches.

Epictetus and Marcus Aurelius for the dichotomy of control. The outside world is weather. The inner citadel is the only jurisdiction worth governing. The Bhagavad Gita for *nishkama karma*, action without attachment to outcome, as the only sustainable operating mode for high-variance work. Ship the imperfect version. The discomfort of beginning is the signal you're doing something real. Not evidence that you should wait.

John Lilly's biocomputer metaphor from 1975 stays close. The mind is a stored-program machine running code it didn't write. Most of what I experience as "my thinking" is inherited from family, from culture, from the attention economy. The work isn't to escape every program. That's impossible. The work is to know which ones are running and which I've actually chosen.

Iain McGilchrist on the divided brain. The Master (right hemisphere, context, presence, the new) increasingly suppressed by the Emissary (left hemisphere, abstraction, control, the already-known). Modern life over-rewards the Emissary. Most of what feels broken about contemporary work is downstream of that inversion.

The reading lives in margins and in essays I publish here. The aim is practical, not academic. How to choose what to care about. How to remain useful without becoming busy. How to act decisively without certainty I'll never have.
