A personal study of how capital moves — across rates, currencies, commodities, and the businesses caught between them. Not as a profession yet, but as a long-form practice of paying attention to what the world is doing.

The framework is Dalio's Big Debt Cycle, currently Stage 6 — late-cycle dynamics where the tools that worked in prior decades progressively stop working. Against that backdrop sits my active thesis: the Cyber-AI Paradox. The consensus reads AI as a threat to cybersecurity revenue. The structural reality is the opposite — AI expands attack surfaces, lowers the barrier to launching attacks, drives a 4.8 million-person workforce gap, and turns every "vibe-coded" startup into a customer creation event for runtime security. The bear case loses either way the AI-offence story goes.

Piketty's *r > g* lens shapes the asset allocation logic underneath. In a world where returns on capital consistently exceed economic growth, the question is not whether to own assets but which ones compound cleanly. Compounding itself I read with healthy scepticism — it works arithmetically, but the lived version is hostage to starting capital, liquidity shocks, and the gap between expected and realised returns. The middle-class compounding narrative is incomplete at best when delivered to people without buffers.

The practice is mostly reading: central bank minutes, sell-side notes treated as primary sources rather than gospel, a small circle of independent writers. Positions, when taken, are sized for sleep, not for fireworks. The aim is not to be right more often. It is to be wrong less expensively, and to keep a written record of the difference.
