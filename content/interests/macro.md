A personal study of how capital moves. Across rates, currencies, commodities, and the businesses caught between them. Not a profession yet, but a long-form practice of paying attention to what the world is doing.

The framework is Dalio's Big Debt Cycle, currently Stage 6. Late-cycle dynamics, where the tools that worked in prior decades progressively stop working. Against that backdrop sits my active thesis, the Cyber-AI Paradox. Consensus reads AI as a threat to cybersecurity revenue. The structural reality is the opposite. AI expands attack surfaces, lowers the barrier to launching attacks, drives a 4.8 million person workforce gap, and turns every vibe-coded startup into a customer creation event for runtime security. The bear case loses either way the AI-offence story goes.

Piketty's *r > g* lens shapes the asset allocation logic underneath. In a world where returns on capital consistently exceed economic growth, the question isn't whether to own assets. It's which ones compound cleanly. I read compounding itself with healthy scepticism. It works arithmetically, but the lived version is hostage to starting capital, liquidity shocks, and the gap between expected and realised returns. The middle-class compounding narrative is incomplete at best when delivered to people without buffers.

The practice is mostly reading. Central bank minutes, sell-side notes treated as primary sources rather than gospel, a small circle of independent writers. Positions, when taken, are sized for sleep, not fireworks. The aim isn't to be right more often. It's to be wrong less expensively, and to keep a written record of the difference.
