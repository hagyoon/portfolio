---
title: Independent Horology
caption: Watches as craft objects
---

I take watches seriously, mostly the independents working at the edges of mechanical horology where finishing is philosophy and not marketing. The current collection leans toward design intent over corporate production. A Baltic Aquascaphe, the French microbrand diver. A Kuoe Royal Smith prototype. A vintage Universal Genève White Shadow Date. The Cartier Tank Must in black lacquer.

The grail shelf is patient. Berneron sits at the top, because the silhouette and dial-side movement architecture is unlike anything else being made right now. FP Journe for the integrity of the resonance and chronomètre work. De Bethune for the avant-garde microtechnology, and for Denis Flageollet's refusal to be ordinary. Akrivia, Naoya Hida, Hajime Asaoka, Laurent Ferrier. The next tier of independents, whose work rewards close attention.

A strap is a system, not an accessory. The Delugs years taught me that pairing is half the watch. Alligator for formal weight, chèvre for fine elegance, sailcloth for casual rigour, rubber for Singapore's humidity. A wrong strap flattens a thoughtful watch. A right one elevates a forgettable case into a daily companion.

The deeper interest is structural. The independent watch market is one of the few consumer categories left where time, judgement, and craft are still priced higher than scale. That's becoming rarer in every other category I look at. Worth paying attention to.
