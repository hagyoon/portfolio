title: Energy & Commodities
caption: Physical assets, asymmetric trades

A side practice of paying attention to the energy complex — oil, uranium, hydrogen, the geopolitics underneath them. These are not the assets that get the attention from the technology-focused investor circles I sit closest to, which is exactly why they are worth thinking about carefully.

The uranium thesis is the most interesting position I hold in the energy complex. The supply-demand math is unusually clean: structural deficit since 2023, contracted demand growth from China, Europe, and the AI compute build-out, no quick supply response possible after a decade of capex contraction. Kazatomprom and Cameco dominate Western production. The price is around incentive level today; the next leg is path-dependent on demand acceleration.

The oil complex requires reading the geopolitical layer alongside the commodity layer. The Iran action in early 2026 was a textbook case of a *supply shock* — inflationary, fiscal-expanding, yield-positive — versus the standard textbook expectation of a *demand shock*. The mistake of treating "war" as a single category cost a lot of safe-haven Treasury holders meaningful money. The actual question is always which transmission channel dominates, and the answer determines whether you should be long or short duration.

The thread underneath all of it: physical assets behave differently from purely financial ones in late-cycle regimes. Building literacy in that domain is part of what I think the next decade rewards.
