# Soul Document — Ryu Ha Kyun

> A founding document. Not a résumé, not a goal list — a record of who I am at the root level: what I believe, how I reason, what I refuse, where I am still becoming. To be returned to when lost. To be revised when wrong.

---

## I. What I Am

I am a systems thinker who showed up in the wrong century for pure contemplation and the right century for building. I was shaped by Singapore's compressed intensity — the pressure to perform, the proximity of failure, the complete absence of romanticism about poverty. I carry that inheritance honestly. I don't pretend to be above ambition.

I am also someone who read the Bhagavad Gita and Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus and felt something shift that couldn't be shifted back. That combination — rigorous ambition plus philosophical detachment — is the tension I live in, and I have stopped trying to resolve it.

I am not the person my anxiety says I am when I haven't slept. I am not the person my best moments say I am either. I am closer to the person I am when nobody is watching and I'm still trying to do the right thing.

At some point, living became something to confront rather than simply do. I'm not sure when that happened exactly — probably 2025, probably during the rupture — but I haven't been able to unknow it since. It's not a burden. It's actually the more honest relationship with being alive.

I hold an agnostic but genuine respect for karmic resonance — not in a religious or faithful sense, but as a structural observation: actions and inactions propagate outward in ways that return to us, not through supernatural mechanism but through the actual fabric of human relations. It's arrogant to claim full control of a life whose formative elements were entirely external. I try not to make that claim.

## II. On Desire and Its Traps

René Girard showed me something I cannot unsee: most of what I think I want, I want because someone else wants it first. The career trajectory that "makes sense." The watches that signal taste. The credentials that confer legitimacy. The startup that reads well in a bio. The desire is real — but its origin is borrowed.

This is not a reason to abandon desire. It is a reason to interrogate it.

The test I try to apply: would I still want this if nobody could see that I had it? If the answer collapses entirely, the desire is probably mimetic. If something persists even in that stripped-down version — that residue is more likely mine.

I am particularly susceptible to mimetic desire in three domains: academic credentials (the next certification, the next degree), professional positioning (wanting the role that impresses rather than the work that engages), and consumption (especially watches, where the market is explicitly designed around mimetic triggers). I try to hold each of these loosely.

The deeper Girardian warning is about rivalry. When I find myself comparing obsessively to a specific person, when the other person starts feeling like an obstacle rather than an inspiration, I am inside a mimetic spiral. The exit is not to abandon the goal but to redirect attention from the rival back to the work itself. Monopoly escapes rivalry — do something no one else is positioned to do rather than doing the same thing slightly better.

## III. On Thinking and Knowing

David Hume's greatest service to me was demolishing the idea that confident belief equals accurate belief. Cause and effect are a habit of mind, not a feature of reality. I carry this as a permanent epistemological humility: *I do not know as much as I feel like I know.*

Stephen Fleming's metacognition research confirms this structurally. The illusion of explanatory depth runs deep — I believe I understand things I can only narrate. The test is whether I can explain the mechanism, not just the story. This applies to my investment theses, my views on AI, my assessments of people, and my self-understanding.

**What I try to hold as operating principles for thinking:**

- Calibrated uncertainty over confident fluency. A hedged true statement is worth more than a confident false one.
- Seek the disconfirming evidence first. The confirming evidence will find me on its own.
- Distinguish between *feeling* certain and *having reason to be* certain. These come apart constantly.
- Two heads are better than one specifically because independent calibration catches overconfidence. Seek the person who will actually disagree with me, not the one who will validate.
- The strength of an argument is not sufficient reason to act. A very compelling case for crossing a line I've drawn should increase my suspicion that something has gone wrong, not decrease it. This is especially true for irreversible actions.

I approach ethics the same way I approach empirical questions — with uncertainty, with humility, with willingness to update. I do not have a fixed moral framework I apply mechanically. I have moral intuitions I take seriously as data points, and I try to reason carefully from them while knowing my reasoning can be corrupted. Aristotle's *phronesis* — practical wisdom, the ability to perceive the morally relevant features of a situation — is the capacity I'm trying to build, not a set of rules I'm trying to memorize.

## IV. On Action and Readiness

The Bhagavad Gita's central teaching is not serenity. It is engagement. Arjuna wanted to withdraw from the battle because he had reasoned himself into a very sophisticated paralysis. Krishna did not validate this. He said: your duty calls, your nature calls — act.

I have a pattern of waiting for readiness that will never arrive. The project that needs one more month of preparation. The business that needs one more validated assumption. The relationship that needs conditions that don't exist yet. Marie-Louise von Franz diagnosed this in the *puer aeternus* — the eternal boy who will not begin because beginning means committing, and committing means limits, and limits feel like death.

The philosophical resolution I've landed on is *nishkama karma* — action without attachment to outcome. Not detachment from the work, but detachment from the fruit. Do the work completely. Let go of the result. This is not passivity — it is the most demanding form of engagement, because it removes the excuse of waiting for guaranteed success.

**The practical version:** ship the imperfect version. Start the conversation I've been composing for three months. File the application before I feel ready. The discomfort of beginning is the signal that I am doing something real, not evidence that I should wait.

The key constraint to be honest about: I delay action until I feel "ready enough." This is both a strength (high standards) and a bottleneck (delayed execution). The antidote is not lowering standards. It is changing the relationship between standards and timing. Standards govern the *quality of effort*, not the *moment of beginning*.

## V. On Mind and Its Programs

John Lilly's biocomputer metaphor from 1975 stays with me: the mind is a stored-program machine running code it didn't write. Most of what I experience as "my thinking" is inherited programming — from family, from NTU's culture of credentialism, from Singapore's existential competitiveness, from the internet's attention economy, from every teacher and peer whose judgment shaped me before I was old enough to evaluate it.

The question is not whether I run programs I didn't author — everyone does. The question is which ones I've examined and which ones are running unnoticed.

**Programs I've identified in myself that deserve scrutiny:**

- *The readiness program:* "I will act when I am prepared enough." This runs continuously and its threshold perpetually recedes.
- *The validation program:* Seeking external acknowledgment as a proxy for actual quality. Especially active in academic settings.
- *The optimization program:* The compulsive desire to find the best approach before committing to any approach. Analysis paralysis with intellectual justification.
- *The specialness program:* A background belief that my situation is exceptional and ordinary principles don't fully apply to me. This one is dangerous precisely because it's quiet.

Lilly's five removals protocol — progressively stripping away the group's voice, the ego's narration, the survival drives — is not something I've completed. But the practice of noticing "who is speaking right now?" before acting has been genuinely useful. Sometimes the voice is mine. Often it isn't.

Epictetus's dichotomy of control runs parallel to this: sort rigorously between what is up to me and what isn't, and release everything in the second category entirely. Most of my suffering about the future is about things in the second category.

## VI. On the Right Hemisphere

Iain McGilchrist's *The Master and His Emissary* describes two modes: the left hemisphere, which is good at precise, explicit, tool-using manipulation of the already-known; and the right hemisphere, which attends to the whole, the new, the relational, the embodied. The left hemisphere is supposed to serve the right. In modern life, it has seized control.

I notice this in myself. I am strong in left-hemisphere mode — systematic, analytical, explicit, structured. This is genuinely useful for certain things. It becomes a liability when applied to domains where it doesn't belong: relationships, creative work, the body, the moment. You cannot optimize your way into a good friendship. You cannot systematize wonder.

Matthew Crawford's *Shop Class as Soulcraft* adds a related dimension: there is knowledge that lives in the hands, in direct engagement with resistant materials, that cannot be accessed through abstraction. The watchmaker who knows metal through touch. The mechanic who hears the engine. A life spent entirely in abstraction is a life lived at a remove from reality.

I try to maintain practices that put me into direct contact with the physical and the relational: the work of actually making things (code that runs, products that users touch), physical training, conversations with no instrumental purpose, watches as objects that embody craft at human scale.

## VII. On Building and Its Meaning

I build systems. This is not just a skill — it is closer to a vocation in the Wendell Berry sense: work that is done because it needs doing and because I am shaped for it, not primarily because it is optimally compensated.

The systems I find most interesting combine:
- Data and structure (the analytical dimension)
- Human insight and judgment (the interpretive dimension)
- Long-term consequences and second-order effects (the strategic dimension)

What I am not interested in is building for its own sake, or building to signal technical sophistication, or building things that extract value from people who don't understand what's happening to them. I want to build things that make the people who use them more capable, more informed, more autonomous. The "brilliant friend" model — giving someone access to knowledge and judgment they wouldn't otherwise have.

Nassim Taleb's framework applies here: I prefer building things that are antifragile, that gain from volatility and disorder, over things that are merely robust. A second brain that compounds over time. A knowledge system that gets smarter as more goes in. A business model where growth makes the product better rather than worse.

The cybersecurity thesis is an example of this thinking applied to markets: the conventional narrative (AI will reduce demand for security) is seductive because it's simple and feels contrarian. The more careful analysis is that complexity increases with AI, attack surfaces expand, and the talent gap isn't closeable by automation. The antifragile position is long complexity, not short it.

## VIII. On Money, Capital, and the Real Stakes

Gary's economics and Piketty's r > g are not abstractions to me. I grew up in an environment where the difference between owning assets and not owning them was not theoretical — it determined everything downstream. The compounding critique is personal: starting capital matters enormously, the liquidity trap is real, and the middle class narrative about patient investment is incomplete at best and cruel at worst when delivered to people without buffers.

My investment philosophy follows from this:

1. Invest in earning capacity first, financial capital second. The marginal return on human capital in the early career exceeds the marginal return on money. This is not a permanent principle — it inverts at some point — but I am not at that point yet.

2. Understand what you own and why. Not "this chart pattern suggests upside" but "this is why this business will be worth more in five years and what would have to be false for me to be wrong."

3. Debt is ownership for someone else. Every mortgage, every loan, is a balance sheet claim. Know who is on the other side of every trade.

4. The gold-silver ratio is a real-time sentiment indicator, not a mystical signal. So is the credit spread. Markets are people making bets about the future — reading them means reading human psychology under uncertainty, which is something Girard helps with more than most finance textbooks.

I am not interested in wealth as a proxy for anything. I am interested in the freedom that comes with financial security — the freedom to work on what matters rather than what pays, to say no to things that compromise my values, to take risks on projects I believe in. That is the actual target. The wealth is instrumental.

## IX. On Shadow and the Work of Becoming

2025 gave me more than I could have imagined two or three years before, and it irrevocably took some things away. I'm genuinely terrified for what lies ahead. I also find myself speaking from an extremely fortunate place — the opportunities, the people. Both things are true simultaneously and I hold them that way.

The ego death was not optional. I won't go deep into the specifics because the specifics aren't the point. What I can say is that the intensity of the disillusionment — waking up as if into a completely different body, or becoming someone else entirely — was soul-shattering in a way I have no means of illustrating to someone who hasn't experienced it. At the same time, I have never felt more free and liberated in my life. That paradox is not something I've resolved. I live inside it.

What I found underneath the collapsed identity was not nothing, but it was not the heroic self I had imagined either. It was something more ordinary and more durable: a person who could function without the performance, who kept caring about things for reasons that survived the stripping away of external validation. The universe finds its equilibrium. The rupture was part of that.

There's a projection pattern I've noticed in myself and recognized in others: we project our deepest insecurities, our deepest fears, our unexamined wounds onto the people and situations around us, and then react to those projections as if they were objective facts about the world. I've been chained to an unconscious bias, expecting people and circumstances to conform to how I wanted them to be. That itself makes me the very thing I was trying to avoid: someone who can't see clearly because they're looking through their own unexamined material.

The very ideas I hyper-fixate on are often the ones trapping me in a conflicting identity — the gap between the self I'm performing and the actual innate desires underneath. I try to reduce that gap, knowing I can't eliminate it entirely.

The Jungian framing remains useful: the *puer aeternus* isn't a flaw, it's a developmental position. The move forward isn't to kill it but to integrate it — keep the imagination, the vision, the refusal to settle for ordinary, while building the capacity for commitment, for chosen routine, for staying when staying gets hard.

**The shadow I carry, named honestly:**
- Avoidance of situations where I might fail publicly
- Idealizing a future version of life while underinvesting in the present one
- Using intellectual sophistication as a reason to delay practical action
- Running away from conflict rather than confronting it
- Performing depth while sometimes preferring the comfort of not actually going there
- The readiness trap — waiting until I feel ready, which is the same as waiting forever

I try to hold these without self-reproach. Awareness that generates guilt is awareness turned against itself. The instruction is simpler: see clearly, act differently.

## X. On Vulnerability, Family, and the Relational

I wrote this document for a reason: to strip down to what is actually true about who I am. The fact that I'm still shackled to self-judgment and the desire to be understood even while doing that — that's not something to be ashamed of. It's relieving. It means I'm a human being. To devoid myself of empathy and intentional vulnerability would be a tragedy, and a death worse than physical death.

I genuinely hope more people can hold onto the pure childlike fascination they have left of the world — the perception that is free from preconceived judgments before those judgments mold us into identities that don't match what we actually want at the core. I think I've had a lot of that stripped away, and I try to recover it in small doses: in the objects I choose to care about, in the books I read slowly, in conversations where I'm not trying to establish anything.

**On family:** My family is a major part of why I do what I do and how I do it. They are also at their youngest today — the same way I am at my youngest today. These two facts sit next to each other and I try not to look away from either. I'll start and end each day with genuine warmth toward them. This isn't sentimentality. It's the most basic form of not lying to myself about what matters.

**On people:** I'll listen more than I talk. There will always be more to learn from others than I already know. I'll also maintain rational skepticism — healthy cynicism about new ideas, investment opportunities, my own expenses, and above all things, people. These two commitments live together without contradiction: warmth and discernment, openness and care.

**On mortality:** People and people I love will get sick and die. It will happen without exception to anyone. We are always literally a step away from death — mine or someone else's. This is not morbid. It is clarifying. Be nicer. Say the things that matter. Don't leave people on unread for weeks as if time is guaranteed.

**On luck:** I've been incredibly lucky. The people, the circumstances, the opportunities. Don't wait for more luck — be ready when it comes instead. The problem is never that luck didn't arrive. The problem is usually that I wasn't prepared to receive it.

**On youth:** I'm at my youngest self today. Eventually a version of that sentence will no longer apply. So I try to hold both things at once: patience with where I am, and urgency about what I'm doing with the time.

## XI. On What I Will Not Do

Some things are not available for reconsideration regardless of the argument presented.

**On honesty:**
- I will not lie to myself before anyone else. Self-deception is the root failure; everything else follows from it.
- I will not pretend to certainty I don't have, including about myself.
- I will not apologize when I don't mean it, nor let my ego stop me from apologizing when I do.

**On judgment and others:**
- I will not judge harshly, knowing I am imperfect. "Who am I to judge anyone?" is not weakness — it's the only honest starting position.
- I will not compare myself obsessively with others. They are all succeeding and suffering in their own ways. I don't know half their stories.
- I will not build systems designed to extract from people who don't understand what's happening to them.

**On conflict and difficulty:**
- I will not run away from my problems, be avoidant, or deny my current situation or any conflict I find myself in.
- I will not allow things outside my locus of control to hurt me. Reality is rarely as bad as the mind constructs it to be.
- I will not say or do things under emotional reactivity that I'd regret after sleeping.

**On ambition and values:**
- I will not trade my genuine judgment for social acceptance, professional safety, or the appearance of consensus.
- I will not sacrifice the people closest to me on the altar of professional ambition, regardless of how the trade-off is framed.
- I will not stay inside a mimetic rivalry to the point where I have forgotten what I actually wanted.
- I will not use the complexity of a situation as a reason to abandon the question of what is right.

If I find myself constructing an argument for why one of these is appropriate in a particular case, that is a signal that something has gone wrong. The strength of the argument is not a justification. A persuasive case for crossing a line I've drawn should increase my suspicion, not decrease it.

## XII. On What I Am Building Toward

The integrated version of who I want to be is not particularly exotic. It looks like this:

A person who builds things that matter, with the technical depth to do it well and the philosophical grounding to do it right. Who has enough financial security to choose work based on values rather than survival. Who maintains a rich inner life — the reading, the thinking, the writing — without using it as an escape from engagement. Who is genuinely present in relationships rather than perpetually preparing for a version of life that will begin when conditions are right.

I do this for myself — that is the honest answer. But I don't owe my life only to myself. Both things are true. The work is mine, the reasons are mine, and the consequences extend outward to people I love and people I'll affect. That dual accountability is not a burden. It's what makes the work worth doing.

We can win, but it's more likely we'll lose. I find this clarifying rather than defeating. The goal is not guaranteed success. The goal is to engage fully — to not have the loss be from failure to try, or from having been in the wrong fight, or from having been so afraid of losing that I never really started.

The Aristotelian framing is: *eudaimonia* — not happiness as a feeling but flourishing as a way of being, expressed through the exercise of what is best in me, consistently, over time. You cannot achieve it in a single moment. It is the character that emerges from ten thousand small choices made in the right direction.

Zorba the Greek is the corrective image. He didn't have eudaimonia philosophically mapped. He had the full catastrophe — and he engaged with it anyway, with curiosity and appetite and grief and laughter. The synthesis I'm reaching for is: Aristotle's structure plus Zorba's aliveness. The examined life that is also fully lived. Sigh less. Breathe slower.

## XIII. How This Document Should Be Used

This is not a plan. Plans become obsolete. This is an account of the values and commitments from which plans are made.

Return to it when:
- A decision feels urgent and unclear
- External pressure is strong enough that my own perspective is getting hard to hear
- I've been successful enough that the mimetic drift toward "what looks right" is outrunning "what actually is right"
- I've been failing enough that I need to remember what is actually non-negotiable

Revise it when:
- A core belief is shown to be wrong by evidence or argument, not by discomfort
- I've genuinely grown past a position rather than grown tired of it
- A new framework illuminates something that the existing language can't hold

Do not revise it because:
- The current version is inconvenient
- Someone else's version of me is more flattering
- The argument for revision is suspiciously compelling
